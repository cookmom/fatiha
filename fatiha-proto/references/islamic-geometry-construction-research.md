# Islamic Geometric Pattern Construction Methods — Research

## 1. Compass-and-Ruler Construction (Traditional Method)

### Fundamental Principle

All Islamic geometric patterns derive from circles. A compass and straightedge are the only tools required. The circle is the "primordial symbol of unity and the ultimate source of all diversity in creation." Every pattern begins by drawing a circle and dividing it into equal parts.

### The Three Symmetry Families

Patterns fall into three main families based on how the circle is divided:

| Family | Division | Base Angle | Key Shapes | Multiples |
|--------|----------|------------|------------|-----------|
| **Four-fold** | 4 parts | 90° | Squares, octagons | 4, 8, 16 |
| **Five-fold** | 5 parts | 72° | Pentagons, decagons | 5, 10, 20 |
| **Six-fold** | 6 parts | 60° | Triangles, hexagons, dodecagons | 3, 6, 12 |

### Step-by-Step: Six-Fold Pattern (Easiest Entry Point)

1. Draw a circle with compass
2. Without changing the compass radius, place the point anywhere on the circumference
3. Draw an arc that intersects the circle
4. Move compass point to new intersection; repeat around the circle
5. Six equally spaced points appear (the compass radius = circle radius, so it divides into exactly 6)
6. Connect alternate points → equilateral triangle
7. Connect the other alternate points → second triangle rotated 60°
8. Overlap = six-pointed star (Seal of Solomon / Star of David)
9. Connect all adjacent points → regular hexagon

### Step-by-Step: Eight-Pointed Star (Octagram / Khatam)

1. Draw a circle
2. Draw a horizontal diameter through the center
3. Construct a perpendicular bisector (vertical diameter) using compass arcs
4. Four points on the circle where diameters meet = vertices of a "static" square
5. Connect these four points → first square (resting on its side)
6. Draw the diagonals of this square through the circle center
7. Where diagonals intersect the circle = four more points
8. Connect those four points → second square rotated 45° ("dynamic" square)
9. The two overlapping squares form the eight-pointed star

Alternative (from a square grid):
1. Draw a square
2. Draw both diagonals
3. From each corner, set compass to the distance from corner to center
4. Mark two points on adjacent sides of the square where this arc intersects
5. Repeat from all four corners → 8 points on the square's edges
6. Connect these 8 points → octagon
7. Connect every other point → octagram (8-pointed star)

### Step-by-Step: Five-Fold / Ten-Fold Pattern

**Key angle**: 360° / 5 = 72°. All five-fold angles are multiples of 18°:
- 18°, 36°, 54°, 72°, 108°, 144°

**Pentagon construction:**
1. Draw a circle with horizontal diameter
2. Find the midpoint of the radius (between center and right edge)
3. From this midpoint, draw an arc from the top of the vertical diameter to the horizontal diameter
4. Where this arc meets the horizontal line = a key distance
5. Use this distance as compass radius from the top of the circle → marks two pentagon vertices
6. Step around the circle with this edge length to find all five vertices
7. Connect → regular pentagon

**Decagon:** Bisect each side of the pentagon's circumscribed circle arcs to get 10 equally spaced points.

**The 54° Rectangle:** The standard unit-of-repetition in traditional five-fold Islamic patterns. Used to determine the size and location of every line in the pattern.

### The Golden Ratio (φ ≈ 1.618) in Five-Fold Patterns

- φ is intrinsically related to the number 5
- cos(72°) = (φ - 1) / 2 = 0.309...
- cos(36°) = φ / 2 = 0.809...
- The diagonal of a regular pentagon divided by its side = φ
- Segment lengths in Islamic star motifs consistently align with φ at various scales

### The Five-Circle Grid (Foundation for Tiled 4/8-Fold Patterns)

Also called the "√2 pattern":
1. Draw a horizontal line; place a circle on it
2. Find the perpendicular bisector
3. Draw four circles centered at circle-bisector intersections (same radius)
4. Add eight more circles at existing circle intersections
5. Continue to 25 total circles in a 5×5 grid
6. Draw all diagonals connecting centers
7. This grid generates: tiled dynamic octagons, "Breath of the Compassionate" (8-pointed stars + crosses), and tiled static octagons

### Proportional Division Methods (Mughal / Persian)

**Octagon approximation (Mughal method):** Uses the numerical relationships 7:12:17:24
- An octagon with width 17 has sides of 7
- A right triangle with sides 12 has hypotenuse ~17
- Error: less than 0.1% (~1cm per 10 meters)

## 2. The Polygonal Technique (Primary Historical Method)

### Overview (per Jay Bonner)

The polygonal technique is the primary method used by master artists throughout Islamic history. It employs an underlying **polygonal tessellation** (sub-grid) upon which pattern lines are placed. Once the pattern is generated, the sub-grid is discarded — it is invisible in the finished work.

### How It Works

1. **Create a tessellation** of regular and semi-regular polygons that tiles the plane
2. **Place pattern lines** within each polygon, emerging at specific points on polygon edges
3. **The contact angle** — the angle at which pattern lines meet the polygon edge midpoint — determines the pattern family
4. When polygons are assembled, the pattern lines connect across edges to form the visible design

### The Four Pattern Families (by Contact Angle)

| Family | Contact Angle | Character | Example |
|--------|--------------|-----------|---------|
| **Acute** | 36° | Sharp, spiky stars | Common in Seljuk work |
| **Median** | 72° | Balanced, classic look | Most common family |
| **Obtuse** | 108° | Broad, open stars | Late Timurid patterns |
| **2-Point** | Two symmetric points per edge | Complex interlacing | Advanced Mamluk work |

### Mathematical Basis

- All angles in the pattern are multiples of the base symmetry angle
- For 10-fold patterns: all angles are multiples of 36° (π/5 radians)
- For 8-fold patterns: all angles are multiples of 45° (π/4 radians)
- For 12-fold patterns: all angles are multiples of 30° (π/6 radians)

## 3. Girih Tiles — The Five-Tile System

### The Five Shapes

All sides have the **same length**. All angles are **multiples of 36°**.

| Tile | Shape | Interior Angles | Symmetry |
|------|-------|-----------------|----------|
| **Decagon** | Regular 10-gon | 10 × 144° | 10-fold rotational (36° rotation) |
| **Pentagon** | Regular 5-gon | 5 × 108° | 5-fold rotational (72° rotation) |
| **Rhombus** | Diamond | 72°, 108°, 72°, 108° | Bilateral through 2 axes |
| **Elongated Hexagon** | Irregular convex | 72°, 144°, 144°, 72°, 144°, 144° | Bilateral through 2 axes |
| **Bow-tie** | Non-convex hexagon | 72°, 72°, 216°, 72°, 72°, 216° | Bilateral through 2 axes |

### Decorative Lines (Girih Lines)

Each tile is decorated with internal lines that intersect the center of each edge at **72° and 108°**. When tiles are placed edge-to-edge, these lines connect seamlessly across tile boundaries, creating the visible pattern. "Girih" means "knot" in Persian.

### Historical Development

- Used since ~1200 CE
- Major breakthrough at the **Darb-i Imam shrine, Isfahan (1453)**: patterns achieved quasi-crystalline Penrose-like tilings, 500 years before Western discovery
- By the 15th century, the tessellation approach was combined with **self-similar transformations** to construct nearly perfect quasi-crystalline patterns

## 4. The Yazdi Bandi Method (یزدی بندی)

### Overview

Yazdi-bandi is a Persian technique for decorating the internal surfaces of domes and semi-domes. Part of the broader category of **Karbandi** (three-dimensional ornamental vaulting), which also includes:
- **Muqarnas** — stalactite vaulting
- **Rasmi Bandi** — rib-based dome decoration
- **Kase Sazi** — bowl-making technique

### Historical Development

- Began developing in Iran in the **mid-10th century**
- Constructed during the **Il-Khanid dynasty (13th century)** and later
- Found in Isfahan, Yazd, Kerman, Natanz — in mosques, madrasas, bazaars

### Key Components

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Stars** (ستاره) | Star-shaped geometric units at intersection points |
| **Pabariks** (پاباریک) | Elongated connecting elements between stars |
| **Cells** | Small arch-shaped pieces made from stars + pabariks; the fundamental construction unit |
| **Sine Fakhri** | The most significant geometrical element common to all Yazdi bandi; a specific geometric unit |
| **Takht** | Horizontal star-shaped polygon (in one type only) |
| **Rows/Layers** | The dome consists of concentric rows of cells |

### Construction Method (2D Schema → 3D Dome)

1. **Draw concentric circles** (representing the dome's layers from apex to base)
2. **Place stars on these circles** at regular intervals
3. **Connect stars together** with pabariks
4. **Draw rasmi-bandi** (the rib framework)
5. **Draw shamseh** (sun disc — the central medallion at the dome apex)

### Structural Classification (Two Types)

- **Type 1:** No horizontal elements; purely three-dimensional structural form
- **Type 2:** Contains horizontal star-shaped polygon (Takht); cannot be considered load-bearing

### Relationship to Rasmi Bandi

Yazdi bandi **evolves from Rasmi bandi** through geometric fragmentation, resulting in unique structural and decorative properties.

### Mathematical Collaboration

Mathematicians like **Abu al-Wafa al-Buzjani (940–998 CE)** contributed significantly, facilitating the applicability of complex geometrical designs in structural contexts.

## 5. Eric Broug's Approach

### Background

- MA in History of Islamic Art and Architecture from SOAS, London
- Author of two print books + one ebook with step-by-step instructions
- Founder of the **School of Islamic Geometric Design** (online)
- Runs workshops and lectures worldwide

### Core Method

Broug's approach emphasizes that these patterns require **no mathematical calculations** — only compass and straightedge, following the same techniques craftsmen have used for centuries.

### Published Works

1. **"Islamic Geometric Patterns"** (available in English, Farsi, Turkish, French) — 19 patterns from significant Islamic monuments, with construction steps
2. **"Islamic Geometric Design"** (Thames & Hudson) — hundreds of color photos; historical + practical analysis; chapters on fourfold, fivefold, sixfold, and combined patterns
3. **"Draw Islamic Geometric Star Patterns"** (ebook)

### YouTube Tutorials Include:

- How to draw a sixfold star pattern
- How to draw a tenfold 15th-century Quran page from Cairo
- How to build a muqarnas (3D Islamic geometric design)

### Teaching Philosophy

Design elements created from straight lines and circles are placed in grids, then repeated and varied to generate "seemingly limitless arrays of breathtaking patterns." The key insight is that infinite complexity emerges from simple, repeatable operations.

## 6. Historical Construction & Transmission Methods

### Abu al-Wafa al-Buzjani (940–998 CE)

- Wrote **"Kitab fima yahtaju ilayhi al-sani min ilm al-handasa"** (On the Geometric Constructions Necessary for the Artisan)
- Contains **over 100 geometric constructions** including for a regular heptagon
- Promoted Euclidean "point-joining" constructions with straightedge and compass
- Criticized artisans for using approximations instead of precise geometric methods
- Key contribution: precise methods for **polygon dissection** (cutting and reassembling shapes)
- His dissected square motif appears throughout Iranian mosques including the **Friday Mosque in Isfahan**
- Surviving copies: Arabic original (lost), three Persian translations; most complete Arabic version in Istanbul's Ayasofya library

### The Topkapi Scroll (late 15th / early 16th century)

- **30 meters long**, housed in Topkapi Palace Museum, Istanbul
- Contains **114 individual geometric patterns** for wall surfaces and vaulting
- Believed to have been prepared in **Safavid Persia**
- Includes incised "dead drawings" showing **underlying sub-grids** from which visible patterns are generated
- Does NOT contain written instructions — it is a visual pattern book
- Studied by **Gulru Necipoglu** (1995 monograph via Getty Publications)
- Necipoglu argues these patterns constituted a "sign system" reflecting religious currents, mathematical advances, and mystical beliefs

### The Hasba Method (حسبة) — Moroccan Tradition

- Widely adopted by Moroccan master craftsmen (**Maalam**)
- Based on geometric rules and the concept of symmetry
- **Starts with n-fold rosettes** as the generative center
- Can theoretically generate **all 17 wallpaper groups** (crystallographic symmetry groups)
- In practice, Moroccan geometric woodwork (14th–19th century) mainly uses 5 wallpaper groups: p4mm, c2mm, p6mm, p2mm, p4gm
- Knowledge passed **master to apprentice** — methods were private domain of artisans
- Applied to wood, plaster, metal, marble

### Zellij Tile Construction (Moroccan)

1. Glazed square tiles (~10cm per side) are fabricated
2. Cut **by hand** into a variety of pre-established shapes (memorized by the craftsman)
3. Shapes are combined to generate complex patterns
4. The repertoire of shapes and their combinations = the hasba method in material form

### Pattern Transfer Techniques

- **Pouncing:** A pricked drawing is placed on the target surface; a fabric pouch filled with charcoal dust (pounce) is dabbed over it; dust seeps through holes leaving dotted outlines
- **Full-size cartoons:** Patterns drawn at 1:1 scale on floors or grounds before transfer
- **Pattern scrolls:** Like the Topkapi Scroll — visual reference books carried between sites
- **Formers:** Templates set at specific angles with markings, used to establish construction points; handed down between generations
- **Set squares:** Egyptian craftsmen used 36°/54°/90° and 72°/18°/90° triangles with T-squares to construct decagons without compasses

### Anonymous Treatise (~1300 CE, Tabriz)

- **"Fi tadakhul al-ashkal al-mutashabiha aw al-mutawafiqa"** (On Interlocking Similar or Congruent Figures)
- From the Ilkhanid period
- Emphasizes both puzzle-solving and decorative applications of geometric dissection

## 7. Key Mathematical Relationships (Quick Reference)

### Angle Table for Pattern Families

| n-fold | Full rotation / n | Key angles (multiples) |
|--------|-------------------|----------------------|
| 4-fold | 90° | 45°, 90°, 135°, 180° |
| 5-fold | 72° | 36°, 72°, 108°, 144° |
| 6-fold | 60° | 30°, 60°, 120°, 150° |
| 8-fold | 45° | 22.5°, 45°, 67.5°, 90° |
| 10-fold | 36° | 18°, 36°, 54°, 72°, 108°, 144° |
| 12-fold | 30° | 15°, 30°, 60°, 90°, 120°, 150° |

### Key Proportions

- **√2 ≈ 1.414** — diagonal of a unit square; foundation of 4/8-fold patterns
- **√3 ≈ 1.732** — height of equilateral triangle with unit side; foundation of 6/12-fold patterns
- **φ (phi) ≈ 1.618** — golden ratio; foundation of 5/10-fold patterns
- **cos(72°) = (φ - 1)/2 ≈ 0.309**
- **cos(36°) = φ/2 ≈ 0.809**

### The Three Irrational Numbers and Their Pattern Families

| Irrational | Value | Pattern Family | Grid Type |
|-----------|-------|----------------|-----------|
| √2 | 1.41421... | 4-fold, 8-fold | Square grid with diagonals |
| √3 | 1.73205... | 6-fold, 12-fold | Triangular/hexagonal grid |
| φ | 1.61803... | 5-fold, 10-fold | Pentagonal/decagonal grid |
