# Muqarnas Research Notes

Comprehensive research on Islamic 3D geometric vaulting (muqarnas/stalactite vaults).

---

## 1. What Muqarnas Are

### Definition
Muqarnas is a three-dimensional architectural ornament consisting of tiered, projecting niche-like elements stacked in corbelled layers, creating honeycomb or stalactite-like vaulting. The term may derive from the Greek *koronis* (related to "cornice"). In Persian, muqarnas are called **ahoopay**. They are sometimes referred to as "stalactite vaults" or "honeycomb vaults."

Titus Burckhardt describes them as "cupola supports in the form of niches, repeated one after the other in the same way as the cells of a honeycomb are repeated."

The muqarnas holds the same foundational importance in Islamic architecture as the Ionic column holds in Greco-Roman architecture.

### Core Function: The Square-to-Dome Transition
One of the primary architectural functions of muqarnas is to bridge the transition from a **square base** (the walls of a room) to a **circular dome** above. This is the classic "squinch problem" in architecture. The muqarnas evolved from the **corbelled squinch** -- a wood or stone element placed at interior corners to mediate between rectangular walls and circular domes.

During the Fatimid dynasty, squinches filled upper corners to form octagons or eight-pointed stars. As domes grew taller, architects composed multiple squinches together, creating three-dimensional patterns. The muqarnas is essentially a subdivision and refinement of this basic corbelled squinch into hundreds or thousands of individual cells.

### The Structural vs. Decorative Debate
This is one of the central debates in Islamic architectural scholarship:

**The structural argument:**
- Muqarnas originated as load-bearing squinch systems that distributed dome weight
- Paradoxically, "the more ornate it is, the more weight it can hold" -- each additional tier creates greater surface area, adding increased support
- Early muqarnas (10th-11th century) served genuine structural purposes at stress-concentrated corners
- The brick-and-mortar muqarnas of Iran and Iraq were integrated into the building structure

**The decorative argument:**
- Muqarnas are "not a structural support system" -- they must be hung or attached as interior ceilings, or placed upon supporting roof structures
- Many later muqarnas (especially plaster/stucco examples in North Africa) are purely ornamental
- Their main function became "to obscure or fill the structural transitions within a building"

**The consensus view:**
The muqarnas is **both** structural and decorative, with the balance varying by period and region. It fills zones of transition while simultaneously copying and elaborating the same structural composition. In Islamic architecture, form and function are described as "synonymous" -- unlike in Western architecture where they are often treated separately. Muqarnas remained in use across the Islamic world even after they became structurally obsolete, thanks to their beauty and flexible design.

### Five Essential Characteristics (per scholarship)
1. **Three-dimensionality** -- provides volume in structures
2. **Variable volume** -- functions as both structural support and ornamental device
3. **Infinite scalability** -- "knows no logical or mathematical boundaries"; complexity limited only by architect skill
4. **Dimensional flexibility** -- 3D units can be represented as 2D forms (plan projections)
5. **Stacked tier system** -- composed of cells, each featuring facets and a roof structure

### Theological/Symbolic Meaning
- **Cosmic symbolism**: The square base represents earth, the circular dome represents heaven; muqarnas mediate the earthly-divine transition
- **Occasionalist theology**: The dome appears "to stand without visible support," expressing the Islamic view that "the continued existence of anything is dependent on the will of God"
- **Divine creation**: Scholar Yasser Tabbaa argues the division of the dome into segments implies a conception of the universe -- colors, shapes, and luminosity are "accidents in this world subject to continuous change according to the will of God"
- **Heavenly symbolism**: The eight-pointed star patterns represent Islam's concept of the eight gates of heaven

## 2. Mathematical Structure

### Al-Kashi's Definition (15th Century)
**Ghiyath al-Din Jamshid al-Kashi**, the great Timurid mathematician, provided the foundational mathematical treatment of muqarnas in his treatise **"Miftah al-Hisab" (The Key to Arithmetic, 1427)**. This forms the third and last section of chapter nine, "Measuring Structures and Buildings," in Book IV "On Measurements."

Al-Kashi's definition: A muqarnas is a ceiling-like structure with **facets** and a **flat roof** where every facet intersects the adjacent one at either:
- A **right angle** (90 degrees)
- **Half a right angle** (45 degrees)
- **Their sum** (135 degrees)
- Or **another combination of these two**

Key terminology:
- **Cell**: Two facets together with their roof
- **Tier**: Adjacent cells which have their bases on one and the same surface parallel to the horizon

Al-Kashi used decimal fractions to calculate the total surface area of muqarnas types. His stated motivation: "The specialists merely spoke about this...for the arch and the vault...But I present it among the necessities...because it is more often required in measuring buildings."

### The 2D Plan Projection System
This is the foundational design method for muqarnas:

1. **Every muqarnas begins as a 2D drawing** -- a plan-view projection showing the layout of all cells as seen from directly below
2. The 2D projection presents a **network of lines delineating cell outlines**
3. This 2D plan serves as the **blueprint** for constructing the 3D model
4. The absence of overlapping elements in muqarnas makes it possible to project the three-dimensional structure onto a plane surface "rather easily"
5. The design starts with **a drawing of a two-dimensional tessellation with concentric rings**
6. Each ring denotes a **different tier/layer** in the final structure
7. Rings subdivide into distinct shape-types, each corresponding to specific 3D sculptural elements

### Cell Types and 2D Plan Elements
Al-Kashi classified the basic elements by geometry. The standard 2D projection vocabulary:

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Square** | Base unit |
| **Half-square** | Division of square |
| **Rhombus** | Diamond shape; angles include 45-degree multiples |
| **Half-rhombus** | Division of rhombus |
| **Almond** (badam) | Pointed oval derived from rhombus |
| **Biped** (large) | Derived from the square |
| **Biped** (small) | Derived from the rhombus |
| **Jug** (jag) | Derived from the square |
| **Barley kernel** | Narrow pointed element |
| **Deltoid** | Kite-shaped element |

The large biped and jug are derived from the square; the small biped and almond are derived from the rhombus. These are the simplest elements, all based on the square and rhombus and their divisions.

### 3D Cell Structure
In three dimensions, a muqarnas consisting of six layers includes:
- **Almond cell**
- **Triangle cell**
- **Deltoid cell**
- **Intermediate triangle cell**
- **Intermediate deltoid cell**
- **Biped cell**
- **Triped cell**

Each 3D cell consists of:
- A **base** (the plan-view footprint)
- **Sides/facets** (the visible surfaces, typically curved or angled)
- A **roof** (which may be flat or curved)

### Tier Organization
- Adjacent cells with bases on the same horizontal plane form a **tier**
- Tiers are stacked vertically, each projecting slightly forward (like corbels) from the one below
- Each successive tier is offset and/or rotated from the tier below it
- The angle between blocks in combinations is indicated as a **multiple of 45 degrees**

### The N-gon Tile System (Modern Analysis)
A powerful modern mathematical framework derives muqarnas tiles from regular polygons (N-gons):

**Using a 32-gon system:**
- Each muqarnas tile appears as a **rhombus in plan view**
- All tile edges maintain identical length in both plan and 3D dimensions
- Number of unique tiles = N/2 for even N-gons, (N-1)/2 for odd N-gons
- The 32-gon system produces **16 unique tile types**
- Each tile is identified by angular measurement: Tile 1 = 11.25 degrees, Tile 4 = 45 degrees, Tile 8 = 90 degrees, Tile 16 = 180 degrees
- Tiles 1-15 are curved three-dimensional forms; **Tile 16 is flat and vertical** (critical structural role as "equator")
- Seven pairs of tiles are identical in plan view but differ in 3D positioning: 1/15, 2/14, 3/13, 4/12, 5/11, 6/10, 7/9
- "Each edge is identical, allowing all edges of all muqarnas tiles to be joined seamlessly to every edge"

### Aggregation Methods
Muqarnas elements can be aggregated in two main ways:
1. **Tessellation logic** -- repeating pattern fills
2. **Radial logic** -- emanating from a central point (typical for domes)

### Symmetry Systems
- Simple muqarnas: **4-fold symmetry** (square tessellations)
- Complex muqarnas: **5-fold, 6-fold, 7-fold, or greater symmetry**
- As tessellation complexity increases, so does the number of distinct sculptural element types required
- The cellular pattern is "infinitely scalable" due to its geometric tessellation basis

### Fractal Properties
The tiered honeycomb patterns can be likened to a fractal -- "a shape whose form is determined by a simple algebraic equation and can conceivably stretch to infinity." The recursive, self-similar nature of muqarnas tiers exhibits fractal-like branching from a central geometric point.

## 3. Classification Systems

### The Two Major Traditions

There are two fundamental distinct forms of muqarnas design:

#### A. North African / Maghribi / Andalusian Muqarnas
- **Materials**: Plaster (stucco) and wood; never structural brick
- **Character**: More intricately creative; "tend to not have a standard set of regulations regarding composition, components, and construction"
- **Design approach**: Freeform, highly inventive, each composition potentially unique
- **Visual emphasis**: Extreme visual complexity, elaborate ornamentation
- **Cell composition**: Series of downward triangular projections
- **Notable feature**: Manipulation of light and shadow
- **Key examples**: Alhambra (Granada), Qarawiyyin Mosque (Fez), Great Mosque of Tlemcen (Algeria)

#### B. Iranian / Persian Muqarnas (Ahoopay)
- **Materials**: Bricks covered with plaster or ceramic clay; later colorful tilework and mirror glass
- **Character**: Geometric precision and complexity; "more uniformed style"
- **Design approach**: Built using a **standard set of components and guidelines**; more systematic/rule-based
- **Visual emphasis**: Integration with polychrome mosaic tiles (turquoise, cobalt, navy blue)
- **Cell composition**: Connecting tiers of segments
- **Notable feature**: Multi-tiered compositions combining decorative and structural functions
- **Mirror innovation**: From the 18th century, covered with mirror glass mosaics (earliest: Chehel Sotoun, Isfahan, restored 1706-7)
- **Key examples**: Jameh Mosque Isfahan, Ali Qapu Palace, Shah Mosque Isfahan

#### C. Turkish / Ottoman Muqarnas
- **Materials**: Stone and marble
- **Character**: Continuation of Seljuk tradition; diminished in importance during Classical period (16th century)
- **Design approach**: Became one element in a wider decorative repertoire, not the dominant feature
- **Application**: Mainly entrance portals, niches, column capitals
- **Notable feature**: Painted muqarnas in domes and semi-domes
- **Key examples**: Suleymaniye Mosque, Blue Mosque (Sultan Ahmed), Seljuk caravanserais

#### D. Egyptian / Syrian Muqarnas
- **Materials**: Stone (large blocks)
- **Character**: "Much larger" than Persian counterparts; "concentrated in a limited space"
- **Design approach**: Elements "easily interconnect into the walls around them" for stronger structure in earthquake-prone regions
- **Structural note**: The stone construction and wall integration suggest genuine structural purpose

#### E. South Asian Muqarnas
- **Materials**: Sandstone and marble
- **Character**: Blend with floral and calligraphic motifs
- **Key examples**: Qutb Minar (Delhi)

### Takahashi Classification
Japanese scholar **Shiro Takahashi** has catalogued over 2,000 muqarnas examples worldwide, with 1,536 drawings covering 3,273 muqarnas compositions. He classifies them into three categories:
1. **Square style**
2. **Pole table style**
3. **Other styles (Sinan)**

His database is accessible at: http://www.shiro1000.jp/muqarnas/

## 4. Key Historical Examples

### Alhambra, Granada, Spain (Nasrid Dynasty, 14th century)

#### Hall of the Two Sisters (Sala de Dos Hermanas)
- **Original Arabic name**: al-Qubba al-Kubra ("the Great Dome")
- **Date**: Built by order of Mohammed V, 14th century
- **Dome**: One of the most magnificent muqarnas domes in all of Islamic art
- **Cell count**: At least **5,000 cells** (some sources say over 5,000)
- **Structure**: Cells unfold from a central summit downward into **sixteen miniature domes** around the dome's perimeter
- **Dome diameter**: 8 meters (second-largest dome in the Alhambra)
- **Material**: Stucco/plaster
- **Form**: Rises into a domed ceiling in the shape of an **eight-pointed star**
- **Light**: Receives light from lateral small windows, creating an effect described as "a beautiful and exquisitely rich flower"
- **Function**: Originally housed the Nasrid ruler's wife and family; the poet Ibn Zamrak composed verses about this dome, inscribed on tile skirting boards with metallic iridescence
- **Named for**: Two large twin marble flagstones in the floor, not any legend

#### Hall of the Abencerrajes (Sala de los Abencerrajes)
- **Location**: Opposite side of the Court of the Lions from the Hall of Two Sisters
- **Dome form**: Muqarnas dome in the shape of an **eight-pointed star** opening over eight horns
- **Structure**: Dome rests on **eight pendentives of muqarnas**
- **Illumination**: 16 latticed windows
- **Symbolism**: Eight-pointed star represents the eight gates of heaven (per Yasser Tabbaa)
- **Preservation**: Retains original muqarnas dome

### Ali Qapu Palace, Isfahan, Iran (Safavid, early 17th century)
- **Commissioner**: Shah Abbas I; expanded by Shah Abbas II
- **Height**: 48 meters, six stories
- **Location**: Western side of Naqsh-e Jahan Square, opposite Sheikh Lotfollah Mosque
- **The Music Room** (6th floor): The most famous feature
  - Vaulted ceilings of mud brick with painted, carved stucco and **cutout muqarnas**
  - Double-walled design with plaster niches cut in shapes of **vases and other vessels**
  - **Acoustic innovation**: The muqarnas and cutout shapes function as a **sound diffuser** and **Helmholtz cavity absorber** -- the various vessel shapes create different air volumes behind them, decreasing reverberation
  - Walls decorated by renowned Safavid painters including Reza Abbasi
  - The Music Room represents a unique fusion of architectural acoustics and muqarnas decoration

### Shah-i-Zinda, Samarkand, Uzbekistan (Timurid, 14th-15th century)
- **Complex**: Over 20 mausoleums and cult buildings on the slope of ancient Afrasiab
- **Muqarnas**: Made of earthenware with molded decoration and turquoise glaze (first half of 15th century)
- **Dome muqarnas**: Soft turquoise color; small pointed niches stacked in stair-like tiers below domes
- **Tilework**: Portal decoration includes exquisite tile mosaics (geometric and floral), glazed brick, Kufic and Thuluth inscriptions, and muqarnas
- **Significance**: Demonstrates the evolution of Timurid tilework in Central Asia, from early carved terracotta to painted maiolica to cut-tile mosaics
- **World Heritage**: Inscribed in UNESCO World Heritage List

### Blue Mosque (Sultan Ahmed Mosque), Istanbul, Turkey (Ottoman, 1609-1616)
- **Architect**: Sedefkar Mehmed Agha (student of Sinan)
- **Central dome**: 23.5 meters diameter, 43 meters above floor
- **Dome system**: Central dome supported by 4 massive "elephant foot" pillars, surrounded by 4 semi-domes, each flanked by 3 smaller semi-domes/exedrae
- **Muqarnas placement**:
  - Stucco muqarnas covering transitions between exedrae and supporting walls/arches
  - Muqarnas semi-vault over exterior courtyard entrance
  - Muqarnas semi-vault and inscription panel over prayer hall entrance
  - Marble mihrab with muqarnas vault marking the direction of Mecca
  - Painted muqarnas throughout domes and semi-domes
- **Function**: Mehmed Agha used muqarnas to create "a softer progression from the rectangular outer walls to the round central dome"

### Other Notable Examples
- **Shah Mosque, Isfahan**: Semi-domed grand entrance iwan, 27 meters high, muqarnas covered with polychrome mosaic tiles (turquoise, cobalt, navy blue); "one of the greatest examples of color play on a grand scale"
- **Chehel Sotoun Palace, Isfahan**: Hall of Mirrors with muqarnas semi-domes adorned with mirror pieces (one of earliest mirror muqarnas, restored 1706-7)
- **Qutb Minar, Delhi**: Brick minaret with balconies supported by stalactite muqarnas; inspired by Minaret of Jam, Afghanistan
- **Imam al-Dawr Shrine, near Samarra, Iraq** (1080-1090 CE): One of the earliest surviving muqarnas domes
- **Qarawiyyin Mosque, Fez**: North African tradition
- **Great Mosque of Tlemcen, Algeria**: Combination of structural functionality with elaborate ornamentation
- **Suleymaniye Mosque, Istanbul**: Ottoman muqarnas for transitioning between structural elements

## 5. Digital Reconstruction and Computational Approaches

### Key Papers and Research

#### "Interactive Modeling of Muqarnas" (Yaghan, ResearchGate)
- Algorithms to create 3D muqarnas geometry from 2D plan
- Matching 2D cell plan and neighboring properties with possible 3D cell units
- Focus on Moroccan-Andalusian style muqarnas

#### "Computational Interpretations of 2D Muqarnas Projections in 3D Form Finding" (ASCAAD 2016)
- Generates new 3D geometry from a given or generated planar projection
- Uses **Rhinoceros** 3D modeling software, **Grasshopper** visual scripting, and **Python**
- Demonstrates the 2D-to-3D pipeline computationally

#### "A Computer-Aided Design Tool for Muqarnas" (ACM Journal on Computing and Cultural Heritage, 2024)
- Dedicated CAD tool specifically for muqarnas design
- Published in JOCCH (Journal on Computing and Cultural Heritage)

#### "A Recursive Algorithm for the Generative Study of Seljuk Muqarnas in Kayseri and Sivas" (Nexus Network Journal, Vol. 25, pp. 751-772, 2023)
- Novel approach for computational analysis and parametric modeling
- Studies four octagonal muqarnas structures from 13th-century Anatolia
- Identifies common generative rules:
  - **Recursive stacking** of geometric layers
  - **Fractal patterns** and hierarchical branching of symmetry axes
  - Proportions based on the **silver ratio** (1 + sqrt(2), approx 2.414)
- Combines **photogrammetry, 3D modeling, symmetry analysis, and graph theory**

#### "Computational Modeling and Analysis of Seljukid Muqarnas in Kayseri" (ACM JOCCH, 2022)
- Analyzes 12 unique muqarnas structures
- Shows Seljukid muqarnas exhibit patterned branching of symmetry axis between layers

#### "Reimagining Muqarnas" (CAADRIA 2024)
- Contemporary parametric generative exploration

#### "Structural Muqarnas: Reconstructing Muqarnas Using Graphic Statics" (2023)
- Applies graphic statics methods to understand/reconstruct muqarnas structurally

#### "Geometric Decomposition and Algorithmic Model" (ScienceDirect, 2024)
- Algorithmic model of Ankara Cenabi Ahmet Pasha Mosque muqarnas
- Geometric decomposition approach

### Three Digital Modeling Workflows (Yazar & Dincer, IJAC 2021)
Comparative study using the Sultanhani Caravanserai (1229) muqarnas:

1. **Element-based workflow**: More analytical; examines individual geometric components and relationships
2. **Tessellation-based workflow**: More generative; better for creating variations and exploring design possibilities
3. **Block-based workflow**: More performative; explains underlying structural principles, valuable for fabrication logic

### Software Tools
- **Rhinoceros 3D** (Rhino): Primary 3D modeling platform
- **Grasshopper**: Visual programming / parametric design plugin for Rhino; enables non-programmers to develop muqarnas algorithms
- **Python**: Scripting for algorithmic generation
- **Photogrammetry tools**: For capturing existing muqarnas geometry
- **openNURBS**: Open-source toolkit for 3D geometry transfer

### Expansion Methods for Computational Generation
From the N-gon tile system analysis, identified expansion methods for growing muqarnas compositions:
- **Ray method**: Distinct rays guide placement of successive tiers
- **Crescent method**: Single unique crescent form with alternating layer direction
- **Flame method**, **angle method**, **zigzag**, **lightning**, **zipper**, **jagged**, **snake** methods
- Most methods (except ray) display **chirality** -- right-handed and left-handed pairs

### Computational Dome Variants
Using tile-based systems, researchers have generated:
- Traditional domes (radial N-gon rotation)
- **Onion domes** (using flat vertical Tile 16 as "equator")
- **Flattened domes** (Chrysanthemum, Whirlpool, Dragon Teeth, Rising Star)
- **Seed method domes** (selecting tile arrangements at apex; Peacock, etc.)
- **Muqarnas arches** (seed method mirrored as portal entrance)
- **Spiral staircase ceilings**

## 6. Construction Principles

### The Design Process
1. **2D Plan Drawing (Toomar)**: Craftsmen first drew a 2D pattern -- the horizontal projection of the muqarnas. This is the essential blueprint.
2. **Auxiliary/Reference Lines**: Straight lines or circles guide the 2D layout (visible in the Topkapi Scroll drawings)
3. **Concentric Rings**: The 2D tessellation uses concentric rings, each representing a different tier
4. **Element Selection**: Each ring subdivides into shape-types mapped to specific 3D sculptural elements
5. **3D Assembly**: Individual carved pieces arranged following the predetermined geometric pattern, tier by tier

### The Topkapi Scroll
- **Date**: Late 15th / early 16th century Iran
- **Contents**: Just over 100 drawings, of which **59 involve constructions for muqarnas**
- **Function**: A manual of architectural designs for complex vaults, geometric ornaments, mosaic tiles, and polychromatic masonry
- **Significance**: Proves that 2D layout drawings were prepared before muqarnas were built; the scroll contains auxiliary/reference lines showing the geometric construction process
- **Modern analysis**: Mohammad al-Asad demonstrated via CAD drawings how one of the scroll's patterns could be used to design a three-dimensional vault

### Materials by Region
| Region | Primary Materials |
|--------|------------------|
| Iran, Iraq | Bricks covered with plaster, ceramic tiles; later mirror glass |
| North Africa | Plaster (stucco), wood |
| Syria, Egypt, Turkey | Stone |
| Ottoman Empire | Stone, marble |
| South Asia | Sandstone, marble |
| General | Wood covered with plaster (Africa); plaster casts on wooden frames (later innovation) |

### Assembly Methods
- **Early method**: Hand-carved modules from solid stone, brick, or wood -- extremely labor-intensive
- **Later innovation**: Plaster casts mounted on **wooden frames/scaffolding** -- reduced costs and construction time while enabling flat or curved surfaces
- **Cement**: Used as binding agent along with wood scaffolding
- **Modular nature**: The standardized element types allow flexible adaptation to diverse architectural contexts
- **Decoration**: Modules frequently decorated after assembly with carved patterns, painted surfaces, tiles, or mirror pieces

### Geometric Construction Tools
- **Compass and straightedge**: Classical tools for drawing the 2D plan
- **Dividing base pattern into segments** using geometric tools
- **Segments elevated** into layered 3D structures
- The process required sophisticated understanding of how 2D outlines map to 3D curved surfaces

### The Paradox of Ornament and Structure
A key insight from the encyclopedia.com source: "The more ornate [a muqarnas] is, the more weight it can hold. Each additional tier creates a greater surface area of the structure, adding increased support." This means that in structural muqarnas, the elaboration of decoration was not separate from engineering -- more complexity meant more structural integrity.

### Spiritual Mathematics in Construction
Islamic craftsmen worked with Pythagorean principles: "numbers were the ultimate elements of the universe." The mathematical precision of muqarnas was understood as linking intellectual comprehension with spiritual illumination. The geometry was not merely practical but cosmological.
