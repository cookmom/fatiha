# Islamic Door Design — Comprehensive Reference

## 1. Islamic Geometric Door Panels

### Panel Division Systems
- Doors typically divided into **rectangular panels** arranged vertically, framed by border bands
- Common layout: 2-3 large central panels per leaf, with smaller panels above/below
- Double doors (two leaves) are standard for mosque and palace entrances
- The **Seljuq double door** from Bey Hakim Mosque, Konya (13th c.) measures **182 cm x 116 cm** — walnut wood, carved, partly painted

### Star Patterns on Door Panels
- **6-fold**: hexagonal stars, constructed from overlapping circles — simplest Islamic pattern
- **8-fold**: the most common Islamic star — two overlapping squares rotated 45 degrees; sits inside a 16-fold divided circle
- **10-fold**: historically most common for rosettes — creates overlapping pentagrams
- **12-fold**: hexagonal arrangement — requires two minimal triangles for construction
- **16-fold**: found in Sultan Hassan Mosque doors (1363 CE), Cairo
- Patterns use **girih strapwork** — interlacing bands that weave over-and-under

### Kundekari Technique (Ottoman/Seljuq Woodworking)
- Interlocking wood pieces assembled **without nails or glue**
- Small pentagons, hexagons, diamonds, and triangles form continuous patterns
- Single continuous pattern covers entire panel (not separate tiles placed adjacently)
- Prevents cracking/warping from moisture and temperature changes
- Finest examples from **Anatolian Seljuq** mosques (13th century)
- Also found in **Suleymaniye Mosque**, Shezade Mosque, Mihrimah Sultan Mosque doors

### Panel-Interior Construction
- Great Mosque of Aleppo door: two 12-stars in center (top/bottom of panel), each surrounded by eight 5-stars
- Patterns constructed on grids using only **compass and straightedge**
- Four basic repeat units: (1) circles/interlaced circles, (2) squares/4-sided polygons, (3) star patterns from inscribed squares/triangles, (4) multisided polygons


## 2. Masjid al-Nabawi Doors (Prophet's Mosque, Medina)

### Dimensions
- Each door: **3 meters wide x 6 meters tall** (2:1 height-to-width ratio)
- Thickness: over **13 cm**
- Weight: approximately **1.25 tons** (1,250 kg) per door (other sources cite 2,500 kg per gate assembly)
- Total doors: **100 doors** distributed across four sides, expansions, and roof
- Total gates (bab): **42 gates** providing access to inner complex

### Materials and Construction
- Crafted from **teak wood** — over 1,600 cubic meters across all doors
- Assembled **without nails** using ancient interlocking method (kundekari)
- Each door features more than **1,500 intricately engraved gilded pieces**
- Brass framework **plated with 23-carat gold**, then polished
- Opens effortlessly with one hand despite weight — specialized **pulley system**

### Geometric Patterns
- Gilded pieces arranged into a **circular frame** bearing Arabic inscription "Muhammad, Messenger of Allah"
- Beautiful geometric wooden and metal patterns
- Quranic verse above each gate: "Enter with peace and security" (Surah al-Hijr 15:46)

### Gate Variants
- **Triple-arched gateways**: Bab Quba, Bab Makkah
- **Double-arched gates**: Bab al-Hijrah
- **Single-portal entrances**: Bab Badr, Bab Abu Zar Ghaffari
- **Seven-door gate**: Bab Malik al-Fahad (with two minarets)
- Walls feature **pointed arches with black and white voussoirs**


## 3. Alhambra Doors and Gates (Nasrid, Granada)

### Major Gates
- **Puerta del Vino** (Wine Gate) — dates to Muhammad III, early 14th century
  - Two horseshoe arches (exterior/interior facades)
  - Pointed horseshoe with embossed voussoirs
  - Engraved **key symbol** (important Islamic motif)
  - Spandrels filled with ceramic tile mosaics — interlaced star designs
  - Stucco decoration above arches
  - Symmetrical about vertical axis
- **Puerta de la Justicia** (Gate of Justice)
- **Puerta de las Armas** (Gate of Arms)
- **Puerta del Arrabal** (Arrabal Gate)
- **Puerta de los Siete Suelos** (Gate of Seven Floors)

### Nasrid Design Principles
- All **17 wallpaper symmetry groups** present at the Alhambra — only ancient monument with all 17
- All **7 frieze groups** present in border patterns
- Zellij tilework (cut glazed ceramic pieces) frames doorways and lower walls
- Arches carved from **marble slabs** placed on windows, gates, and entrances
- Materials: rammed earth, lime concrete, brick (structural); gypsum/plaster coatings; marble arches
- Tilework: monochrome glazed ceramic cut with pliers into aliceres (geometric fragment pieces)

### Pattern Characteristics
- Interlaced star-shaped designs (Nasrid style)
- Geometric patterns governed by constructive principles based on geometry
- Patterns generated to produce all possible symmetry groups


## 4. Ottoman Palace Doors (Topkapi Palace)

### Ottoman Door Design Principles
- Synthesizes **Islamic, Byzantine, Persian, and later European** influences
- Emphasis on **compartmentalization**, inward-facing design, hierarchy of access
- Geometric patterns primarily on **minbar (pulpit) and wooden doors** — Ottoman artisans preferred floral/vegetal patterns elsewhere
- Gold ornaments common on palace doors
- Masonry, wood, and tile construction

### Kundekari Woodwork (Ottoman Specialty)
- Some patterns "very simple" — others use "advanced geometric techniques such as geometric inflation or structural design"
- Found in doors of: Suleymaniye Mosque, Shezade Mosque, Sultan Selim Yavuz Mosque, Mihrimah Sultan Mosque (Uskudar)
- Also in Ottoman tombs, doors in Bursa and Edirne
- Interlocking geometric pieces without adhesive

### Decorative Studs and Nails
- Brass or iron studs arranged across wooden door surface
- Status indicated by: scale/thickness of cedar door leaves, fineness of carving, **density of metal studs and straps**, craftsmanship of stone/brick surround
- In Moroccan tradition (related): studs reportedly indicate number of rooms inside
- Stud sizes: dome-headed brass studs available in ~25mm diameter
- Hexagonal grid patterns common — hexagon is one of three shapes that tessellate the plane
- Square grid patterns also used


## 5. Islamic Arch Types — Mathematical Proportions

### General Pointed Arch (Two-Center)
- **Formula**: R = s/4 + h²/s
  - R = radius of each circular arc
  - s = span (horizontal width)
  - h = rise (vertical height to apex)
- Two centers positioned symmetrically on the springing line
- Each arc originates at opposite springing point, intersects at apex
- Centers closer to midpoint → flatter arch; centers farther apart → sharper arch

### Equilateral Pointed Arch
- The most common Islamic/Gothic pointed arch
- Centers located at the **springing points** (each end of the span)
- **Radius = span** (R = s)
- Centers + apex form an equilateral triangle
- Height h = s × sqrt(3)/2 ≈ 0.866 × span
- Ratio height:width ≈ **0.866:1**
- Evolution: from 1/10 span center separation (Qusayr 'Amra, 712-715 AD) to wider separations over time

### Lancet Arch
- Slender, steeply pointed profile
- Rise exceeds half the span (h > s/2 significantly)
- **Radius > span** — centers located beyond the opposite springing points
- Produces narrow, lance-like shape with extreme verticality

### Drop Arch
- **Radius < span** — centers located between the springing points
- Wider, more horizontal emphasis
- Depressed geometry

### Horseshoe Arch (Moorish)
- Arc extends **beyond a semicircle** — sweeps approximately **230 degrees** (50 degrees past semicircle)
- Center of arc is **raised above the springing line**
- **Superelevation** (vertical extension below spring line) ≈ **half the radius** in refined forms
- Opening at base is **narrower** than full span of the arch
- Width at apex exceeds distance between springing points
- Typical spans: **1.22–3.66 m**; rises: **0.72–2.15 m**
- First Islamic use: Great Mosque of Damascus (706-715 CE)
- Most famous: **Great Mosque of Cordoba** (756-796 CE) — double arches with horseshoe (lower) and semicircular (upper)
- Emblematic of Moorish architecture and Mozarabic art

### Ogee Arch (S-Curve)
- Four-centered arch with **S-shaped profile**
- Two concave arcs (lower) transitioning to two convex arcs (upper), meeting at a sharp peak
- Prominent in **Mughal architecture** (Taj Mahal)
- Creates elegant vertical emphasis

### Keel Arch (Fatimid/Ottoman)
- Variant of four-centered arch
- Distinguished by **straight haunches** (vs. curved haunches of pointed arch)
- Tapers sharply at top — "knife-like point"
- Most of the arch's normal radius appears **more straight than curved**
- Characteristic of **Fatimid architecture**

### Four-Centered Arch (Persian/Tudor)
- Two arcs rise steeply from each springing point on a **small radius**
- Turn into two arches with a **wide radius** and much lower springing point
- Enables wider spans than two-centered arches
- Standard form in **wider Iranian architecture** and later **Mughal architecture**

### Multi-Foil Arch (Cusped/Polylobed)
- Multiple circular arcs (foils/lobes) cut into the intrados
- Constructed from **equally-sized circles arranged symmetrically** around vertical axis
- Each foil = arc segment between intersection points of adjacent circles
- Adding more circles = finer cusps, denser pattern
- Nomenclature: trefoil (3), quatrefoil (4), cinquefoil (5), multifoil (5+)
- Earliest known: small mosque at **Qasr al-Hallabat**, Jordan (mid-8th century)
- Famous examples: **Great Mosque of Cordoba** maqsura arches (10th c., al-Hakam II)
- Characteristic of Moorish and Mughal architecture


## 6. Door Panel Rosette Construction

### Anthony J. Lee Method — Step by Step

#### Circle Division
- Rosettes always use **2n divisions** of the circle, where n = fold symmetry
- 8-fold rosette → 16 divisions
- 10-fold rosette → 20 divisions
- 12-fold rosette → 24 divisions

#### Three Proportioning Circles
- **Circle [oa]**: outermost rosette boundary
- **Circle [ob]**: inner star polygon boundary
- **Circle [oc]**: additional petal structure
- Critical relationship: Circle [P1 a] defines point [b] on the radius [oP1]

#### Petal Construction
1. Draw layout circle, divide into 2n parts
2. Establish inter-radii points (P1, P2, etc.) at specific angular positions
3. Points define the **peripheral polygon** matching fold symmetry
4. Bisect angle between polygon side and defining radius → controls petal tapering
5. **Parallel petals**: span full division count (e.g., 8 divisions for 10-fold)
6. **Tapered petals**: reduced span (e.g., 6 divisions) → inward-angling sides

#### Star Polygon
- Using circle [ob] and layout points, draw overlapping star polygon at center
- 10-fold → overlapping pentagrams
- 8-fold → overlapping squares

#### Key Properties
- "Almost all rosettes are basically the same layout, applied to different divisions of the circle"
- Ten tangent peripheral circles around center circle [ob] create five-pointed stars
- The entire pattern reconstructable from a **single minimal triangle**
- Triangle's **incircle** = critical proportioning circle [Pa]

### Fold Symmetries Summary
| Symmetry | Circle Divisions | Peripheral Arrangement | Notes |
|----------|-----------------|----------------------|-------|
| 8-fold   | 16              | Square               | Single minimal triangle |
| 10-fold  | 20              | Pentagrams            | Most historically common |
| 12-fold  | 24              | Hexagonal             | Requires two minimal triangles |

### Historical Appearances
- 8- and 12-point girih rosettes: Alaeddin Mosque, Konya, Turkey (1220 CE)
- Abbasid palace, Baghdad (1230 CE)
- Became widespread across Islamic world thereafter


## 7. Border Band Patterns

### The 7 Frieze Groups (All Found in Islamic Architecture)

| Group | Notation | Symmetries | Description |
|-------|----------|-----------|-------------|
| 1 | p111 | Translation only | Simplest — motif slides along strip |
| 2 | p1m1 | Translation + horizontal reflection | Mirror along strip axis |
| 3 | pm11 | Translation + vertical reflection | Vertical mirrors at half-translation spacing |
| 4 | pmm2 | Translation + vertical + horizontal reflection + 180° rotation | Full symmetry — mirrors intersect at rotation points |
| 5 | p112 | Translation + 180° rotation | Half-turn symmetry only |
| 6 | p1a1 | Translation + glide reflection | Slide + flip — half the translation length |
| 7 | pma2 | Translation + glide reflection + vertical reflection + 180° rotation | Alternating mirrors and rotations |

### Border Pattern Characteristics
- Geometric patterns **tend to be used for borders** in Islamic art
- When in main decorative area, combined with floral designs, arabesques, calligraphy
- Interlaced six-pointed stars especially popular from 12th century
- Patterns incorporate both **translational symmetry** and **point symmetries**
- All 7 frieze types found at the **Alhambra**
- Islamic borders feature: interlocking chains, guilloche (twisted rope), running meanders, zigzag bands, vine scrolls with geometric frames

### Construction Principles
- Based on repeated squares, circles, overlapped and interlaced
- Require only **compass and straightedge** to construct
- Four basic shapes: circles, squares, star patterns, multisided polygons
- Patterns suggest infinite growth and continuation


## 8. Decorative Nail/Stud Patterns

### Grid Arrangements
- **Hexagonal grid**: most common — six overlapping circles as base pattern; regular hexagon tessellates the plane
- **Square grid**: also used, especially in simpler designs
- **Diamond/diagonal grid**: rotated square grid

### Stud Characteristics
- Materials: **brass** (most common for high-status), iron (utilitarian)
- Typical stud size: **~25mm diameter**, dome-headed
- Density indicates status — finer/denser = wealthier household
- In Moroccan tradition: each stud reportedly represents one room inside

### Moroccan Door Studs
- Cedar or walnut wood doors with brass/iron studs
- Studs complement hand-carved geometric patterns and floral motifs
- Combined with: decorative knockers, rich pigments (blue, green, red, ochre)
- Status readable in: scale of cedar door leaves, fineness of carving, **density of metal studs and straps**, quality of stone/brick surround

### Ottoman Stud Tradition
- Less emphasis on geometric stud grids (preferred floral/vegetal decoration)
- Studs used on door frames and strapping
- Geometric metalwork primarily on minbar doors


## 9. Door Knocker (Halqa)

### Iranian/Islamic Gendered Door Knockers
- **Two knockers per door** — different shapes produce different sounds
- **Left side (men)**: thick bar/rectangular/square shape → produces **deep tone**
- **Right side (women)**: metal ring/rounded/heart/bird shape → produces **lighter sound**
- Purpose: host knows visitor's gender before opening → women can adjust hijab accordingly
- **Both knockers alternately** = signal that a group of favorite guests has arrived

### Symbolic Elements
- **Circular backplate** symbolizes the **sun**
- Knocker itself represents the **moon**
- Surrounded by **twelve stars** — protection from bad events
- Overall: cosmic imagery for household protection

### Major Door Knocker Types
1. **Ring (halqa)**: oldest form — metal ring hanging from a boss/backplate
2. **Lion head**: ring hanging from lion's jaws — protective symbolism
3. **Dragon/dragon-lion**: dragons joined by lion's head — ward off evil
4. **Hand of Fatima (Hamsa/Khamsa)**: Moroccan specialty — five-fingered hand shape, protection
5. **Openwork lozenge**: diamond-shaped pierced metalwork on axial boss (stylized lion's head)

### Historical Examples
- Lion head knocker with cock-embellished ring — Islamic Art Museum collection
- Dragon knockers with long pin for door attachment
- Double-knocker doors found throughout Iran, Morocco, Turkey
- Crafted in bronze, iron, or brass

### Placement
- Positioned at approximately **chest to shoulder height** on the door
- Centered horizontally on each door leaf (one per leaf for gendered pairs)
- Single knockers centered on the primary door leaf


## 10. Mashrabiya Screen Patterns

### Five Functional Dimensions (Hassan Fathy, 1986)
1. **Controlling passage of light**
2. **Controlling air flow**
3. **Reducing air temperature**
4. **Raising humidity** of air current
5. **Ensuring privacy** of inhabitants

### Key Geometric Parameters

#### Perforation Ratio (PP)
- Ratio of opening area to total screen area
- PF (Porosity Factor): 1.0 = fully open (no screen); lower = more private

#### Depth Ratio (DR)
- Ratio of perforation depth to width

#### Baluster Diameter/Length Ratio (D/L)
- **D/L = cos(theta_1)** where theta_1 = sun altitude angle
- Determines transition between cooling and heating seasons
- Circular cross-section preferred — gradual shadow minimizes contrast

#### Baluster Offset (Two-Layer)
- Formula: **X1/X2 = 2Y1/(Y1+Y2)**
- Ensures constant solar gain while controlling privacy
- Two-layer lattice = optimal configuration

### Pattern Types
1. **Sadirwan**: standard pattern with turned balusters
2. **Maymoni**: perpendicular mesh — balusters with squared and rounded sections
3. **Cross pattern**: short round balusters assembled diagonally, vertically, and horizontally
4. **Church pattern**: long narrow balusters assembled vertically

### Construction Method
- **Lathe-turned** individual balusters (thousands per screen)
- Assembled **without glue or fasteners**
- Interlocking turned pieces create scalable lattice
- Smaller openings at **eye level** (more privacy), larger openings **above** (more airflow/light)

### Material Properties
- Light oak wood yields ~17% more reflected light than other woods
- Rounded balusters produce lower reflected light than complex geometries
- Larger diameter balusters increase reflected light contribution

### Proportional Variations
- Dense spacing at bottom (privacy from street), open at top (airflow)
- Depth and spacing adjusted for climate, privacy, and aesthetic needs
- Modern designs use two-layer lattices for enhanced winter solar gain and glare reduction


## Proportional Systems in Islamic Door Design

### Golden Ratio (phi = 1.618...)
- Present in Islamic architectural proportions — facades, window placement, column spacing
- Door height-to-width relationships follow harmonic ratios
- Ali Qapu, Isfahan: building width as unity, important points produce phi ratios

### Root-2 Proportion (sqrt(2) = 1.414...)
- Foundation of 4-fold and 8-fold Islamic patterns
- A square's diagonal = sqrt(2) × side length
- Used to generate secondary grid from primary square grid

### Common Door Proportions
- **2:1** height-to-width (Masjid al-Nabawi: 6m x 3m)
- **Golden rectangle** ~1.618:1 for panel proportions
- **Root-2 rectangle** ~1.414:1 for geometric panel frames
- Panels subdivided using geometric construction (compass/straightedge)

### Historical Dimensions
- Seljuq double door, Konya: **182 cm x 116 cm** (ratio ~1.57:1, close to golden)
- Masjid al-Nabawi doors: **600 cm x 300 cm** (ratio 2:1)
- Horseshoe arch spans: **1.22–3.66 m** typical range
